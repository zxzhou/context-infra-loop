# M5. Simplicity Is Cognitive Efficiency

## 1. Core Axiom

Choose the simplest system you will actually run every day, because every extra layer of process steals attention from the work itself. Cognitive resources are scarce, and system complexity grows maintenance cost exponentially. A paper checklist you actually use will always beat a carefully designed tool that requires a startup ritual.

## 2. Deep Reasoning

### 2.1 Competition for Cognitive Resources

Working memory is the truly scarce resource. When your system forces you to remember too much (status, shortcuts, structures, rules, exceptions), it competes directly with real thinking. This is not a marginal cost. It is an exponential cognitive tax. Every added layer of process requires you to maintain another mental model. When these mental models begin to interact, complexity explodes.

In 2013, I gave up a fancy org-mode "time management" configuration because management itself had become the work. The time I spent adjusting shortcuts, designing project structures, and maintaining autogenerated schedules far exceeded the time the system saved me. Worse, every time I looked at that beautiful schedule, my brain split between two questions: "What should I do?" and "What does my system say I should do?" That split is itself cognitive loss. After switching to paper, the workflow became "write it down -> do it," with no intermediary. My brain could focus entirely on the task rather than being distracted by system maintenance.

### 2.2 Friction and Startup Energy

Low-friction tools compound because they require no startup energy. This factor is badly underestimated. A system that requires opening an app, logging in, navigating to the right view, and only then beginning work consumes a bit of willpower every time you use it. That cost accumulates. If you perform that action ten times a day, that is ten times the startup cost. A paper checklist that is always in front of you and always available removes that cost.

This is why I explicitly prefer capabilities such as automatic tracking and backup. RescueTime tracks my time without requiring me to do anything. Dropbox does not require manual backup. Time Machine does not require me to remember backup dates. The value of these tools is not only what they do, but that they do not make me think about them. They work in the background with zero friction. By contrast, a system that requires me to enter data manually every day will gradually be abandoned even if it is more powerful.

### 2.3 Boundaries and Resetting Complexity

Simple systems are easier to keep bounded. This is a key insight. When your system becomes too complex, the only solution is to rewrite it. What is the cost of rewriting? Zero. You take out a new sheet of paper and write your to-do list again. The reset itself is a filter: you write down only what truly matters instead of carrying all historical baggage forward.

By contrast, an endlessly revised grand plan only accumulates process debt. Every time you add a new rule, category, or view, you increase system complexity. Those additions are often irreversible: it is hard to remove a feature that has already been used, because someone may depend on it. The result is a system that becomes increasingly complex, increasingly hard to maintain, and eventually a relic you no longer use.

### 2.4 The Power of Separate Modes

Separate modes matter. Next actions should be frictionless and always visible; big-picture thinking deserves its own time block and its own page. This principle comes from the core insight of GTD (Getting Things Done), but my practice shows that its value goes beyond GTD itself.

What happens when you mix "next action" and "big-picture thinking"? You fall into constant context switching. While executing a task, you suddenly think of a large strategic question and your attention gets pulled away. Or in reverse, while planning at a high level, an urgent small task interrupts you. The switching cost is huge. Research shows that switching from one task to another can take 15-25 minutes before full cognitive ability returns.

The solution is physical separation. My notebook has two parts: one is "today's next actions," always in front of me; the other is "big-picture thinking," which I open only at specific times such as weekends. This separation not only reduces cognitive load, but also lets me switch between modes more cleanly. When I am in execution mode, I know I do not need to think about the big picture. When I am in planning mode, I know I do not need to worry about today's details.

### 2.5 Simplicity in a Multi-Threaded Life

In a multi-threaded, fast-moving life, simplicity becomes even more critical. When you are handling work, family, and several interest projects at once, your cognitive resources are already distributed. In that situation, your system must be extremely simple, otherwise it becomes another thread that needs management.

Analysis of 2024 time records showed that my life involved many domains: parenting, video editing, 3D printing, automation programming, and household repairs. Each domain has its own tasks and deadlines. If I tried to manage all of this with a complex system, the system itself would drown me. Instead, my strategy is to maintain an extremely simple "next action" list and focus each day on only the most important 3-5 items. Everything else goes on a simple "to-do" list, but I do not try to prioritize or organize all of it. This coarse-grained management looks imprecise, but its benefit is that I am never troubled by system maintenance.

### 2.6 Relationship to Other Axioms

Simplicity complements M1 (Closed-Loop Calibration). Closed loops need fast feedback, and complex systems slow feedback down. A simple system lets you see results faster and therefore adjust faster. Simplicity is also related to M4 (Active Management): when your system is simple enough, you can put more energy into active management instead of passively maintaining the system.

Simplicity also relates to X03 (Efficiency Is Determined by Bottlenecks). In a complex system, the bottleneck is often not the work itself, but system maintenance. By simplifying the system, you remove that bottleneck and let the real work become the limiting factor.

## 3. Application Criteria

### When to Use

Use this for personal productivity systems, repeatable workflows, team processes, knowledge accumulation, or any situation where you find yourself spending more time "organizing" than executing. Simplicity is especially necessary in these scenarios:

- **Personal task management**: if your to-do system takes more than 30 seconds to add a new task, it is too complex.
- **Team processes**: when a process takes more than three steps to complete a common task, it is too complex. Team members will start routing around it, creating confusion.
- **Knowledge management**: when your note system makes you spend time "organizing" notes instead of writing them, it has failed.
- **AI collaboration**: when instructions to AI take more than one page to explain, your system may be too complex. Simple instructions often produce better results.

### How to Practice

1. **Keep removing steps**: start with your system and ask: is this step truly necessary? What happens if I delete it? Repeat until the process becomes "write it down -> do it."

2. **Keep one single next-action list**: do not maintain multiple lists, priority systems, or views. One list, with at most ten items per day. If it exceeds ten items, the problem is your priorities, not the need for a more complex system.

3. **Rewrite instead of over-editing when things get messy**: when your system becomes chaotic, do not try to repair it. Rewrite it. Take out a new sheet of paper and start again. This automatically filters out what does not matter.

4. **Use occasional high-level reviews instead of stuffing strategy into daily execution**: do not try to think strategically during daily execution. Schedule a fixed time, such as the weekend, for high-level review. Use that time to think about the big picture, adjust direction, and reprioritize. At other times, focus only on execution.

5. **Choose zero-friction tools**: for features you use often, choose tools with no startup ritual. Paper, simple text files, and automatically backed-up cloud storage are all zero-friction. Avoid tools that require logging in, navigation, and configuration.

6. **Review your system regularly**: each month, ask: is my system helping me, or dragging me down? If it is the latter, simplify it. Do not become emotionally attached to a system that does not work.

### Common Traps

- **Over-design**: spending too much time designing the "perfect" system instead of actually using it. An imperfect system you use always beats a perfect one you do not.
- **Feature creep**: continuously adding new features and rules until the system becomes unmanageable. Every time you add a feature, ask whether it is worth the added complexity.
- **Multi-tool syndrome**: using multiple tools to manage the same thing. This scatters information and creates synchronization problems. Stick to one tool unless there is a very good reason.
- **Ignoring friction cost**: underestimating the cost of startup rituals. A system that takes two minutes to start may not sound bad, but if you use it ten times a day, that is 20 wasted minutes.
- **Treating the system as the goal**: people sometimes start optimizing the system itself and forget that its purpose is to help them work. If you spend more time optimizing the system than doing real work, that is a signal that the system is too complex.

## 4. Practical Cases

### Case 1: From Org-Mode to Paper

In 2013, I used Emacs org-mode for time management. The system included:

- multi-level project structure
- autogenerated schedules
- complex tags and priority systems
- hourly time tracking

The system looked powerful, but in practice:

- adding a new task took 3-5 minutes (finding the right project, setting priority, adding tags)
- I spent more time maintaining the system than the system saved me
- I was often troubled by the complexity of the system rather than by the task itself

After switching to paper:

- adding a new task took 10 seconds
- my brain could focus entirely on the task
- when the system became messy, I only needed to rewrite a new list

Result: productivity improved, stress dropped, and focus increased.

### Case 2: Simplifying Team Process

A team's code-review process originally had:

1. Create PR
2. Add labels and milestone
3. Assign reviewers
4. Wait for automated checks
5. Conduct code review
6. Resolve comments
7. Obtain approval
8. Merge

This process had eight steps, and each step had its own rules and exceptions. Developers often skipped steps, causing confusion.

The simplified process:

1. Create PR
2. Wait for automated checks and code review
3. Merge

Only three steps. Labels, milestones, and assignment are automated or unnecessary. The result is that the process is followed more often and runs faster.

### Case 3: Simplicity in Knowledge Management

A common trap is creating a complex note system with many categories, tags, links, and so on. Then you spend time "organizing" notes instead of writing them.

A simpler method:

- one folder, with all notes inside it
- name files by date (YYYY-MM-DD)
- use full-text search to find notes
- do not try to organize in advance

This system looks primitive, but its benefits are:

- adding a note takes 5 seconds
- searching notes takes 2 seconds
- there is no maintenance cost
- over time, notes naturally form patterns

## 5. Deeper Insight

The real value of simplicity is not saving time, although it does save time. It is protecting cognitive resources. In a world of information overload, too many choices, and constant interruption, your attention is the most valuable resource. A simple system lets you concentrate that resource on what truly matters.

This is also why simplicity is especially relevant to a multi-threaded life. When you are handling multiple projects, roles, and interests at once, cognitive resources are already distributed. In that situation, your system must be extremely simple, otherwise it becomes another thread to manage. Simplicity is not a luxury; it is a necessity.

Finally, simplicity is also a philosophical stance. It reflects acceptance of "good enough" rather than pursuit of "perfect." A good-enough system you actually use will always beat a perfect system you do not use. This principle applies to many parts of life, not only task management.
