# M3. Quantified Prioritization: Lift the Main Rope and the Mesh Opens

## 1. Core Axiom

Use rough quantification to find the few leverage points that dominate the outcome. Spend time there first, then polish the rest. Correctness is cheap, easy to overturn, and prone to scattering attention; the real capability is knowing what matters, what does not, and in what order to act.

## 2. Deep Reasoning

### Why Correctness Is Not Priority

"Correct" is often a distraction. It has three fatal weaknesses. First, correctness is hard to define and easy to overturn: every conclusion depends on assumptions, and different assumptions produce different "correct" answers until the debate becomes a fight over definitions. Second, correctness is cheap: non-original knowledge can be acquired from a single explanation and does not require long accumulation. Third, obsession with correctness leads to excessive qualification and hedging, which blurs the core insight and interferes with expression and understanding.

By contrast, social value is not created by compiling a dictionary of absolute correctness. It comes from grasping the main contradiction, focusing on the central goal, and winning the mainstream market. One interesting observation: if a job's main responsibility is ensuring that something is "correct" (junior accountant, junior programmer, translator), it is often among the easiest to replace with AI. That is exactly why correctness itself has low value.

### The Core Mechanism of Quantified Prioritization

The classic example is a full hard drive. There are two approaches: (1) randomly delete one hundred thousand small files by gut feel; (2) use a tool to find the largest file and delete it. The efficiency gap is orders of magnitude. The second method is not better because it is "more correct"; it is better because it identifies the governing rope of the problem, the largest constraint, and concentrates force there. This is the meaning of lifting the main rope so the whole mesh opens: once the governing line is clear, filling in the details becomes much simpler.

This principle appears repeatedly in engineering. The first step of any project should be to define a quantified metric, then optimize around that metric. Otherwise you fall into the pattern of fixing one gourd while another pops up: improve A and B breaks; fix B and C fails. Only a representative quantified metric that synthesizes the whole can preserve situational awareness.

### The Dynamic Nature of Priority

Bottlenecks move. Once you solve the current rate limiter, the next constraint appears. Yesterday's optimization may become today's waste. Quantified prioritization is therefore not a one-time plan; it is a continuous measure-identify-focus loop. After every meaningful improvement, measure again, because the topology of constraints has changed. This is also why the "80/20 set" is dynamic. You must recalculate often instead of relying on an outdated priority list.

### Quantified Prioritization as a Communication Tool

Quantified prioritization is also a powerful communication tool. It lets you say "no" or "not yet" with reasons rather than feelings. When you can point out that one improvement affects 5% of users while another affects 60%, the decision becomes transparent and debatable. That transparency is crucial in teamwork because it turns priority from personal preference into a data-based discussion.

### Relationship to Connection and Verification

Quantified prioritization does not stand alone. It forms a triangle with two other core capabilities: connection and verification. Connection helps you identify which factors truly matter; through cross-domain experience and analogy, you can see bottlenecks others miss. Verification confirms whether your priority judgment is correct; through experiments and feedback, you can adjust direction quickly. Only the three together become a genuinely advanced capability.

## 3. Application Criteria

### When to Use

- **Backlog overload**: you face a long to-do list and do not know where to start
- **Unclear direction**: you face a situation where "what next" is not obvious
- **Firefighting mode**: multiple problems appear at once and resources must be allocated quickly
- **Automation planning**: deciding which repeated tasks deserve automation (answer: if you will do it a third time, it is worth automating)
- **Any decision with more options than time**: this is the normal case

### How to Practice

1. **Define one success metric**: not "better," but a measurable number such as users, latency, cost, or accuracy.
2. **List candidate actions**: all possible directions for improvement.
3. **Estimate impact and cost**: even a rough estimate is better than none. Relative scale matters more than absolute precision.
4. **Choose the top 1-2**: do not try to do five things at the same time.
5. **Timebox execution**: limit time spent on this priority to prevent perfectionism.
6. **Measure again and reorder**: this is the key loop, not a one-time step.

### Common Traps

- **Over-precision**: spending three weeks calculating priority precisely instead of spending three hours estimating roughly and acting immediately.
- **Ignoring dynamism**: setting priorities once and not revisiting them until the project fails.
- **Confusing priority with perfection**: high priority does not mean it must be perfected; it means it should be done first.
- **Single-dimensional optimization**: looking at one metric while ignoring other constraints such as cost, risk, or learning value.

## 4. Relationship to Other Axioms

- **X03 (Efficiency Is Determined by Bottlenecks)**: the goal of quantified prioritization is to find the current bottleneck.
- **M01 (Closed-Loop Calibration)**: quantified prioritization requires a continuous feedback loop for adjustment.
- **M02 (Reverse Debugging Mindset)**: use hypothesis testing to verify whether your priority judgment is correct.
- **T04 (Data Beats Opinion)**: priority should be based on data, not intuition or authority.
- **V02 (Verifiability Is the Foundation of Trust)**: quantified metrics are themselves verifiable.
