# A13. Three Stages of Technology Adoption

## 1. Core Axiom

Adoption of a revolutionary technology is not component replacement; it is system reset. The electric motor took 60 years to reshape the whole economy, not because of the technology itself, but because factories, power grids, and production processes all had to be redesigned. AI is the same.

Technology adoption has three stages: **Driver** (passive use) -> **Co-pilot** (collaborative optimization) -> **Architect** (system reset). Each stage has a different bottleneck, and the solutions are entirely different.

## 2. Deep Reasoning

**Steam-engine thinking vs. power-grid thinking**

In the steam-engine era, a factory used one large steam engine to drive every machine. When electric motors appeared, people initially used them the same way: one large electric motor replaced the steam engine. The real revolution came from the power grid: every machine could be powered independently, factories could be laid out flexibly, and machines could be started on demand. That required entirely different infrastructure.

**Focus shifts across the three stages**

- **Driver stage**: The user passively receives AI output. The bottlenecks are prompt quality and model capability.
- **Co-pilot stage**: The user and AI collaborate and iterate. The bottlenecks shift to data quality, feedback loops, and evaluation criteria.
- **Architect stage**: The system is redesigned around AI. The bottlenecks are infrastructure: documentation clarity, data accessibility, API stability, and evaluation frameworks.

**Infrastructure bottleneck metaphor**

A supercar cannot go fast on a dirt road. In the same way, even the strongest AI model cannot create much value when it meets chaotic data, unclear requirement definitions, and unstable APIs.

**Adoption checklist**

Use this checklist to evaluate whether you are ready to enter the Co-pilot or Architect stage:
- Is the documentation clear enough for AI to understand the business logic?
- Is the data accessible and structured?
- Are the APIs stable, with clear error handling?
- Are clear evaluation criteria defined, instead of vague labels like "useful"?

## 3. Applicability Test

Refer to this axiom when:

- Evaluating adoption of a new technology; first diagnose the current stage, then decide where to invest
- Hearing complaints that "AI is not good enough"; the problem may be infrastructure, not the model
- Designing AI-native systems; prioritize infrastructure investment over chasing model upgrades

## 4. Relationship to Other Axioms

- **T01 Infrastructure Over Components**: Directly aligned. Infrastructure investment has much higher long-term returns than point optimization.
- **A08 Prompt Quality Is the Main Lever**: True in the Driver stage; after entering Co-pilot, data quality and evaluation frameworks gain more weight.
- **A12 AI-Native Development Paradigm**: The concrete practice of the Architect stage: redesigning system architecture around AI capabilities.
